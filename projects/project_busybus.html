Problem

You find an interesting image for future reference. How do you save it? Do you create a google doc with a number of links? You cannot preview them, so when you try to access it- it’s just a sea of links! How frustrating. Sure, you can use Evernote, or Dropbox Paper and Pintrest. But what if you need to add notes move images around, compare them, change sizes, put text on it?

Currently, users need multiple applications to accomplish these tasks.

Solution

Idea Board is a one stop solution for all those needs.

Through comprehensive user research, competitive analysis and user testing, I created Idea Board- a note-taking application that helps you see the big picture and connect the dots. It gives you an infinite canvas and lets you create a note, move it around, and even put it on top of each other.

Ideaboard works the way you think.

Discovery

The stakeholders had mentioned a number of possible features, including, creating content in various formats, saving content they find online, organizing content, sharing documents, collaborating with teammates as well as social sharing. But they leaned on me to help them figure out a clear vision, and narrow down the opportunity and niche market.

When we think about Cloud Storage, some big names immediately come to mind, including Google, Dropbox, Evernote and Microsoft. There are plenty of ways we can go about it, so how do we narrow the focus?

User Survey

To find find under-served market, I first had to figure out HOW people use their current cloud service. I wanted to learn about how people create their documents, how they collaborate, what methods they use to save and organize their files, and how they access them later.

I deployed a user survey to gather data in order to look for patterns, trends and outliers. I conducted phone and in-person interviews, and also created a survey that was completed by 22 participants.

Survey Analysis

Synced devices

Use laptop to create documents and mobile to access it. Google Docs was the most popular application followed by Dropbox and Onedrive.

Focus on note-taking

Save web links and take rough notes on a cloud platform, like google docs and opt to create final presentation on another application.

Enhance preview options

Find it hard to save reference images and videos from web - and even harder to remember exactly what was saved as it lacks image previews.

Competitive Analysis

Cloud storage is a crowded market. There are a number of big-name competitors in the space. To beat them, we first have to know them.

I decided to take a deeper dive into Google Drive, Dropbox and Evernote to examine the positioning, primary audience(s), and their differentiators. My goal was to figure out we can identify why the competition has taken a particular course of action.

The Competition

Google Drive

Google Drive is more broad, so it appeals to a wide market. It has great features for collaboration and inexpensive storage space.

User Pain Points:

Multimedia features aren't robust.

Dropbox

With project management tools like to-do lists, schedules, comment on images, sync, dropbox has cornered the enterprise market.

User Pain Points:

Skimpy free version.

Evernote

Evernote is a popular note-taking application. It makes notes easy to find. It also has a web-clipper and advanced annotating options.

User Pain Points:

No fodlder uploads.

The Opportunity

There is an under served market of Creative professionals. Visual designers, motion graphics artists, producers, directors, editors, etc. If we create collaborative cloud keeping in mind the needs of a specific group, we can provide a lot of value to our niche users.

To brainstorm specific features for the product, I answered How Might We questions using insights from my research through the pov of my user personas.

How might we help Anna create mood-boards?

  • An option to upload images, videos and text anywhere on the board.

  • An option to move the notes around and overlap them.

  • Ability place links to the images and videos directly to the board— and preview them.

How might we help Marc save links from the articles he reads online?

  • Ability to clip articles.

  • Preview an excerpt of links he saves directly to the note.

How might we help Anna organize her notes?

  • Ability to tag notes, images and videos.

  • Filter by tag.

  • Ability to search notes by the date they were created, the type of media they contain (audio, images, etc.).

How can we help Marc give notes to his team?

  • Ability to collaborate adn share with the team.

  • Ability to draw and comment on the photos.

  • Ability to leave a time-coded note on videos.

Validate

I performed one in-person and two remote usability tests on my mid-fidelity prototype to see how users would complete certain tasks once they were given some context. The goal was to identify pain points that could be improved in future iterations.

The users were able to perform all major tasks, but I had a few hiccups for the following.

Notes Tab Menu: Before

The users found it hard to star the notes. You can only see the tab menu on the top of the note- while you're active on the note.

Notes Tab Menu: After

The updated version made the tab visible without having to select the note. It’s greyed out- but visible and becomes functional when the user is on the particular note.

Upload: Before

In the first version, the users could simply drag the files they need to upload on to the canvas. However, some users seemed to be unsure about this feature.

Upload: After

In the updated version, I have included an upload icon on the menu along with the ability to drag and drop to clarify it.

Search: Before

When users searched a keyword- they got results without context. This can get confusing as the number of notes grow.

Search: After

Now search results have context. It tells you if the keyword appears on a board, note, title or body.

Key Takeaways

Idea Board is my first major project where I took client’s vague concept, defined it based on the data and created a working prototype.

My background in content has taught me one thing- it’s all about the story- about the hero’s journey. In this case, the hero is our user. So for me the most important thing was to learn how they do a particular task- what steps they go through- what are the obstacles and how we can make this journey easy for them. Luckily, my background in content has also given me a number of skills like empathy, conducting interviews, analysing micro-reactions, etc that directly helped in creation of this app.

The most important lessons I learned are as follows:

1: I as a designer has to be the problem finder- before I am a problem solver.

Asking right questions is harder than finding the right answers. If we know the problem, there are ways to solve it, test it and make sure it works. However, the biggest issue is figuring out how to ask the right question. From what I gather, the questions should start out being macro- but then, get into the micro. If we want to create a better experience, it is important to take time and understand the step by step process that an user goes through while performing a task.

2:Keep it easy.

As a designer there’s a natural desire to try something out of the box every single time. I come from visual - motion graphics background, and browsing through Dribble and Behance is my hobby. So I was temped to add a lot of bells and whistles to every page of the screen. However, when I did preference tests, I understood that people are inclined to pick what they’re used to, they like it when it’s simple. “Don’t make them think!”

3: Simple is hard

There is truly a paradox of choice. When we try to offer too many options, it is often more difficult for people to choose one. So I, as a designer has to make the right choices, so you don’t have to.

In case of Idea Board, the client was interested in every conceivable option when it came to cloud storage. It was my job to figure out the scope.

I strived for simplicity in wireframes and aesthetics as well. How can I use one step to do this function instead of two. How do I add more context to it? Am I appropriate feedback within reasonable time? Are these colors helping them navigate or distracting? How do I minimize the user's memory load by making objects, actions, and options visible- when they need it.

4: The devil is in the details.

This is true from User Survey to Interface Design. I found myself thinking a lot about small things. I remember asking questions like- “Do you find yourself exporting .pdfs or .jpgs more often?”, or “Where do you find inspiration”, “What is the amount of time you spend on sites like Pintrest, Dribble, Behance, etc” “Where do you save notes from your client phone calls” I also wanted to make sure my survey was taken by my target market. In order to do this without having the resources to pay for it, I found Facebook groups of designers and creatives and posted my survey there. I think it helped me get a lot more focused responses.
